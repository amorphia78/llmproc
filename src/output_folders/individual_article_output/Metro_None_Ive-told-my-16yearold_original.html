I’ve told my 16-year-old we’re too poor for Christmas

I used to spoil my only daughter, Sania – now aged 16 – for Christmas.

We’d decorate our house with lights, buy a large tree, wear matching pyjamas, and even go on an annual trip. We’ve been to the big adventure water parks in Stoke-On-Trent, the Black history museum exhibitions in Liverpool and the pier in Brighton.

Then there were the presents – sometimes up to a dozen for her. One year, she was begging me for an electric scooter, so I saved up months for it and bought one for her.

As a mother, to see the smile on the face of my child when she unwraps her gifts or experiences Christmas joy is everything.

But this year, it won’t be a big occasion. In fact it won’t be an occasion at all.

Christmas is cancelled in our house. The cost-of-living crisis has meant that properly celebrating this holiday for disabled people is simply impossible.

Everything changed for me in 2015.

I was working as an English language teacher, but I had a stroke at the age of 38 and then had emergency open-heart surgery. Sania was only eight then, and it was a tough time.

Unfortunately, the stroke brought on all my current health problems – including diabetes and high blood pressure. Ever since, I use an electric wheelchair and it’s why I have to sleep with oxygen.

I also went into a serious depression, but I didn’t want to accept it. As a result, I locked myself away from everyone and the only person I wanted to see was my daughter. She is what keeps me going.

At the time, she knew mummy wasn’t well, but she didn’t realise how bad until she reached the age she is now.

I couldn’t work so I applied for benefits, which came to about £600 a month.

It was a hard adjustment but you end up having to make do. Until the pandemic, I was managing my money and budgeting accordingly, but we were only just getting by.

In the last few years, however, things have become unmanageable. The cost of everything is so high that we couldn’t afford Christmas last year.

At the time, I explained to Sania: ‘Mummy is broke and has no money’. I told her that things had become so expensive that – until we can afford it – we wouldn’t be able to have Christmas.

She was disappointed, to say the least – she’s still a child after all. But she was understanding.

So last Christmas Day, the strength of my medication meant I just slept. Sania wanted to be near me so she came into my room and she sat with me, shaking me awake so we could watch a film together.

Thankfully, my niece in the US sent her $100 (£80) for her 16th birthday last year, which was in December.

Our financial situation hasn’t improved since then so, unfortunately, we have to cancel Christmas this year too. We’ll spend it the same way as last year.

No mother wants to cancel Christmas, so it feels awful. My spirits are really dampened.

People might tell me to budget better, but how do I do that when there is no budget? Day-to-day, if Saina and I can’t afford food in the shops because we have to pay for the electricity, then we have to put that food back on the shelf.

It’s not just Christmas we are cancelling, it’s life in general – including Sania’s birthday.

To not be able to make days like these special for your child is just unimaginably hard. But there’s nothing I can do.

She’s such a good girl and she says to me: ‘Oh mummy, I want to help you so much, I want a job.’ but she’s at college and it’s so tricky for her to get employed at such a young age.

I know our family is not alone.

Research commissioned by disability charity Scope found that almost one in three disabled people say they expect to be struggling financially this winter – while a third say they will struggle to enjoy Christmas due to the cost.

So I’m trying to use my story to try and raise awareness, and support Scope’s vital helpline for people who are unable to cope.

I’ve been helped by their Disability Energy Support (DES) with advice and a voucher. They gave me £30 to put towards my energy bill when I needed it most.

They also gave me advice about how I can save energy in a practical way at home, like turning off switches and things. It was definitely helpful.

The way I feel now and the life we have is really pushing me to try and go out and work, even though I’m too ill. It’s a desperate situation.

Some people might wonder if I have a message to the Government – but what’s the point? I know they aren’t listening to people like me.

So, until someone does listen, Christmas is cancelled. For me, and many others.

And I don’t know how we’re expected to keep living like this.

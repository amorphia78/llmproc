I did a naked photoshoot at 46 and it cured my low self-esteem

Slipping off my robe, only one thought entered my mind.

‘S**t! I should have shaved.’

It was a silly thought for a number of reasons.

One – because I loved my bush. I’d never been one to care about priming myself or hair removal.

And two – I was stripping off for a naked photo shoot.

I was in a room full of people who I’d never have to see again. So what did I care if they saw my wild pubic hair?

Besides, in just a few months’ time my naked body would be on full display in my local weekly newspaper, and I couldn’t wait.

At 46 I was making a statement: I will not conform. I love my body. And yes, I am allowed to pose naked, no matter what you think.

Up until then, I had spent most of my life obsessing over my body.

In high school, having moved across the country with my dad after my parents’ divorce, I’d taken up cycling.

I became obsessed with the sport, or more accurately, loved that I could drop pant sizes effortlessly.

When I visited my mother a year later, she wrapped her arms around me and cried.

I had lost 20 pounds or more and a mother knows when something’s wrong.

During our visit, my mom bought a video on eating disorders and made me watch it.

Out loud, I denied everything in the video – but inside my head, I checked off many of the boxes.

I wasn’t ready to give it up trying to lose weight, though.

I was still relentless and it didn’t take long for the happiness I felt at losing the weight to be replaced by an overwhelming dislike for my muscular thighs.

I hated not being able to fit into those stupid skinny jeans. I wore skirts that hid my legs and baggy clothes in general to hide myself away.

Things only got worse as I entered my twenties. I under ate; over ate; obsessed about calories.

The whole time, I cycled.

After graduating from journalism school in my early 30s, cycling became the way I saw the world.

It became my livelihood as I forged a career as a travel writer specialising in cycling – riding up mountains throughout Europe, and Peruvian peaks in incredibly thin air.

Those critical thoughts about my body disappeared on these trips.

But back home, I obsessed over gaining a few pounds – and it ruined my day; destroyed my confidence.

Gradually, I started to see my body as my instrument

By my mid 30s, the relationship with my body was still no better.

I wasn’t looking after myself.

I recall being near Paris in 2016 when my legs turned to concrete. I couldn’t move.

Realising I hadn’t eaten that day, I panicked – I was alone and my cycling trip in Europe had just begun – I couldn’t go on like this for months.

If I was going to ride, I had to eat.

Gradually, from that moment on, I started to see my body as my instrument. I had to nurture it.

And, slowly, things started to change.

When I joined a women’s only bike group in Canada in 2015 and started seeing how other women trained, joking about wobbly bits they didn’t like, it helped.

They were strong and healthy and enjoyed life – and I wanted to see my body in the way they saw theirs. Doing so allowed me to live more fully.

I participated in my first mountain bike race in 2015 and hearing the screams of friends as I pushed myself, exhausted and sweating across the finish line, was amazing.

But more incredible than that was the fact that I came in second.

‘You’re stronger than you think,’ my future husband told me. And he was right.

So in 2018, not long after the race and  just after turning  46, when I saw a call out from a local magazine asking people for their body stories, complete with a naked photoshoot, I knew I had to get involved.

I was finally ready to celebrate my body. No more slagging it off or comparing it to others’.

This would be a great way to share what I’d come through, and that now, I saw my body for what it was. Mine.

I had never felt stronger or more emotionally secure in how I looked and what I had become.

Those legs had pedalled thousands of miles, taking me through dozens of countries

Of course, that didn’t stop me from being nervous when I walked in the room.

Because it wasn’t only the shape and size of my body that had become an obsession.

Around the time my mother died in 2017, I began suffering from psoriasis.

A giant red rash developed over both my legs and it made me horribly self conscious, especially in the summer months.

If I wore a skirt or shorts, I was convinced that people would be staring at the red splotches as I walked down the street.

At first I was angry.

But after a while, I became immune to their stares and trained myself to ignore them.

I was nervous going into the photoshoot – my mouth was dry as the make-up artist gently brushed my eyelids with pink eyeshadow, and intrusive thoughts did get the best of me at first, worrying about what people would think.

But once I remembered that I had chosen to do this, I felt liberated.

I lunged into my starfish position and smiled as I heard the editor say: ‘Wow, you’re so incredibly strong. Those legs!’

Those legs had pedalled thousands of miles, taking me through dozens of countries.

They were my trademark.

After the shoot, I rode home on my bike, of course, smiling so widely I thought I might fall off. I couldn’t wait to see the final result.

On New Year’s Day 2019, the day my naked photos were due to appear in the magazine, Now Toronto, I was beside myself with excitement.

I must have spent all morning hitting the F5 button on my keyboard waiting for the site to load, before heading out and buying a physical copy.

Grabbing a paper, I flipped to the page where I finally saw myself.

Wearing only earrings, arms outstretched, nipple and naked thigh on full display I tingled. I had never felt more proud of those giant cyclists’ legs or thankful for those tiny breasts.

Turning to the clerk, I held the picture up to her with glee.

‘This is me!’ I exclaimed. ‘Ohhh nice,’ was her polite reply.  I was ecstatic and I couldn’t wait to show everyone.

Now, I’m 51, and whenever I look at those photos, I still feel gloriously happy. That naked photo shoot was a new beginning for me.

Since then I’ve been to a nude beach for the first time with my partner, and I loved seeing so many other women of all different shapes and sizes all proud in their own skin. I’ve gone to sex clubs and felt completely natural showing off my body, all of it.

It seems silly to me now how much I once cared about how I looked and I’m so glad I’ve embraced myself. We should all aim to do the same.

Your body is the engine that gets you through life. And, when it becomes strong – it’s sexy.

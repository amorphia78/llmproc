I didn’t realise I’d been groomed and trafficked – then the panic attacks started

When I was driven two hours from my hometown to Telford, aged just 16, to have sex with multiple men against my will, I wish I could say it was the first time.

Unfortunately, environments like this had become the norm to me.

In fact, I estimate that after being a victim of human trafficking, I was forced to have sex with at least 1,500 men over the course of five years.

On that day in Telford, myself and my friend Denise*, also 16, got out of the van and were led by her stepfather towards an abandoned flat.

Everywhere I looked, there were men having sex with women and girls.

Denise’s stepfather told us who to ‘start’ with. I complied and started having intercourse with several of the men.

I overheard discussions between the men talking about how much they were charging for our use. Later that afternoon, one even threatened me with a knife.

It felt like just another day in the life of a victim of modern slavery, but now – after fully coming to terms with what happened to me and having a family – I’ve made it my mission to raise awareness and campaign so that what happened to me will never happen to my daughter, or anyone else.

I had a normal upbringing in Wales – my parents were together and both worked good jobs.

But when I started high school, life took a turn. The transition from primary school was hard and I struggled with the workload and making friends.

I rarely went and when I did, I left early. Instead, I hung around my estate – and that made me vulnerable.

My best friend at the time, Katie*, lived nearby and it didn’t take long for her stepdad to pick up that I was easy to take advantage of – that’s where the trafficking began.

He told me, a girl of only 11, that if I delivered drugs for him, he would buy me cigarettes.

I was 14 years old, and my ordeal was only just beginning

I didn’t think anything of it – it was an easy job taking drugs down my top from one end of the estate to the other, getting fags as payment.

Not long after, I was expelled from school for my behaviour and persistent absence, moving to a specialist college where I met my friend Andrea* and things got worse.

Andrea and I spent more time together, and one day she took me to a park behind a kebab shop we visited.

Some men she knew from the shop met us there. Andrea signalled for me to get in their car, and when I did, they took me away and raped me, catching me quickly when I tried to escape.

I felt like I had been ripped apart. I was hurting all over and it felt like my body wasn’t my own. It was a disconnect – almost like adrenaline, until the pain trickled in and then got worse.

I was 14 years old, and my ordeal was only just beginning.

When I shared details of this traumatic event to Andrea, she dismissed me, saying it was ‘just sex’. I was in so much agony afterwards. When we walked home to her house, I dumped my bloodied clothes on the way.

I later found out my friend had been groomed and exploited, and she was doing the same to me.

Traumatised, I used alcohol and drugs (primarily weed and speed) to dull what had happened, but things only got worse.

It seemed like a group of men knew what had happened to me and made me do sexual favours for them all the time in return for something I needed or wanted.

Soon, it was a regular thing. If I needed a taxi, drugs, a drink at the pub, or even a kebab, I had to do some kind of sexual favour – I was simply a target, ripe for exploitation.

My parents just believed me when I told them I was hanging out with friends. In fact, they were really glad I was being social after not being able to easily make friends.

At 16, I was strangled by a drug dealer when I was trying to protect a friend

That’s when things escalated with Denise’s stepfather, who owned a fast food shop that was 15 miles from the centre of town. He would hang out with many men in the same line of work as him.

Eventually, we had been used for sex by many of them – they all knew who we were and that they could use us if they wanted.

Denise’s stepfather knew men all over the country who would pay to have sex with girls – girls like Denise and I. That’s when he came up with the idea to take us to Telford ‘for a cruise’ – except it was just his mate’s flat in Telford.

Before long, my friend and I were routinely taken around the country to have sex with men.

During this time, I was attacked a couple of times. At 16, I was strangled by a drug dealer when I was trying to protect a friend.

I had red marks on my neck and Mum took me to the police station to report it. As far as I know, nothing ever came of it.

Another time, someone threatened to petrol bomb my house. I don’t know why they did it – it was like they just enjoyed inflicting fear and pain. I reported this to the police too and the boy was arrested then made to pay a small fine.

During all my years of exploitation, I was known to the police. I would later find out there were 200 pages detailing how I had been arrested, made reports, been referred to social services, and had others ringing the police concerned about my welfare.

I can’t remember most of it, even though it clearly happened. There was never any significant police intervention though.

Throughout this whole time, I have since estimated (via later writing a book, while working through notes I had made, police reports, and memories) I was raped by over 1,500 men. And I say rape because I couldn’t properly consent.

I started to lose a lot of weight because my mouth and throat were too sore from the abuse to eat. I choked so much when I tried to swallow.

Soon, I was self-harming.

At 20, I found out I was pregnant, but the father wasn’t someone who had abused me – he seemed different from the others. This relationship was short-lived though.

Initially, I was terrified. I said to my parents I was going to have an abortion, but they assured me they would help bring a baby up with me.

When my daughter was born, my life was turned upside down again. But this time, for the better.

I knew my only responsibility was to reform my life for my daughter’s sake – I had to care for her and keep her safe. But it was incredibly hard. I stopped taking drugs, and I cut myself off from everyone who I had spent nearly a decade with, including my friends. It was a very lonely time.

But I got older and stronger, and was committed to starting a new life for my daughter. My trafficking ended, just like that.

For years after my little girl was born, I suppressed everything I had been through.

But one day in 2019, I took her to primary school and saw someone who I’d had run-ins with during the period I was trafficked.

My first ever panic attack soon followed, and I kept having them. I couldn’t figure out what was going on.

Slowly piecing together the events of my past, I requested my police records out of curiosity and started to understand I had been exploited. This is when I found there were over 200 pages of crimes reported from myself and about me.

I reached out to a few organisations and was told to call a helpline, who confirmed that I’d been a victim of modern slavery, and offered support.

I assumed things would get better almost instantly and that I would soon heal – but it has been a long and painful process working through everything I experienced.

That work will never stop, but my priority now is to raise awareness.

I’m working with the Human Trafficking Foundation to train organisations to spot the signs of exploitation in vulnerable young people and am sharing my story as part of Causeway’s campaign to highlight survivors’ lives beyond what they’ve experienced.

Two decades on from the cycle of abuse and the exploitation and those 1,500 men, things are better. I have a stable home and job, and my daughter – who is now a teenager – is protected from suffering the same fate.

Someone told me once that I don’t look like a victim of trafficking.

I know people expect me to look or act weak, but I also know I’m not. I’m so much more than the exploitation I suffered.

I want to make the future better for girls like me, like my daughter. I want to turn my pain into something positive, something powerful.

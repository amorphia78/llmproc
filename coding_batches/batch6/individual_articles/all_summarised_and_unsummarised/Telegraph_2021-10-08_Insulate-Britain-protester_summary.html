Insulate Britain protester Cameron Ford admits 'I'm not perfect' after photos emerge of his 10,000-mile holiday

The eco-activist, who helped to block roads on the M25, drove from Cambridge to Poland in a diesel van after a four-month stint in Canada

Cameron Ford, an Extinction Rebellion and Insulate Britain activist, gained notoriety last month after spending his 31st birthday in a police cell for blocking roads on the M25. The carpenter from Whittlesford, Cambridge, remained unapologetic for the disruption to motorists, commenting at the time: "I hope by causing disruption through civil disobedience that we can get our message across."

Mr Ford's actions were part of a series of protests aimed at disrupting traffic on the M25 motorway. The protests were organized by Insulate Britain, a group demanding that the government insulate all social housing in Britain by 2025 and retrofit all homes with improved insulation by 2030.

However, new photos have emerged reportedly showing Mr Ford indulging in globe-trotting holidays across Canada and Europe. Photographs reveal the activist embarked on a four-month holiday across Canada in the autumn of 2019, potentially involving a round trip of 8,500 miles in flights. It is believed he travelled at least 1,500 miles within Canada during the jaunt, which could amount to a total of 10,000 miles travelled.



When challenged by the Telegraph over his holidays, Mr Ford did not deny the existence of the photos. Speaking from atop the roof of a garden shed, Mr Ford told the Telegraph: "If we start looking for anyone who is not a hypocrite we are not going to solve the problem. I am not perfect, I want a future that is liveable. This is not about me."

He claimed the photos published by the Mail Online were an attempt to "demonise" him. Mr Ford confirmed to the Telegraph he had travelled to Poland with his girlfriend to visit her family.

The article highlights the apparent contradiction between Mr Ford's environmental activism and his personal travel habits, potentially undermining the credibility of his protests and the broader environmental movement.

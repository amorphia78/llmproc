Greenham Common 40 years on - when ordinary women drove nuclear weapons out of UK

Three Welsh protesters reveal what they learnt after being part of the renowned Women’s Peace Camp, the world’s longest-running anti-nuclear demonstration, forty years ago in Berkshire

Nearly 40 years ago, Greenham Common in Berkshire was the site of the world's longest-running anti-nuclear demonstration, the Women's Peace Camp. The protest began in September 1981 when 36 members of a peace movement called Women for Life on Earth arrived at the gates after a 120-mile march from Cardiff. Their numbers soon swelled to 30,000, united in protest against Margaret Thatcher's plans to bring American cruise missiles to Britain.

Three sisters from the Welsh Valleys - Susan Lamb, Lesley Brinkworth, and Christine King - were among the protesters. Susan, then a housewife from the Rhondda, recalls, "I was just a housewife from the Rhondda, terrified what could happen to our children. We had to stand up for what we believed in."

The women faced significant challenges during their protest. They were jailed, threatened by anti-protest vigilantes, and even took US President Ronald Reagan to court. Lesley admits, "I was a different person at Greenham. We learned to face our fears and that if people come together we can change the world."



The protesters lived in cars and tents at Greenham Common, without electricity or running water. In 1982, the sisters were part of a group of 23 women who stormed the entrance of the base and were jailed for 14 days for breaching the peace. In 1983, thousands of women formed a human chain around the airfield in an "Embrace the Base" demonstration.

The protest had a significant impact. Susan states, "Our pressure forced the Government's arm, no doubt about it. We had won." In 1989, the missiles began leaving Greenham Common. The sisters hope their actions will inspire future generations of activists against new world threats like climate change.

The surprising science of climate protests

This week, climate activists from Just Stop Oil made headlines by disrupting the World Snooker Championship, covering the table in orange powder. This disruptive action is part of a series of provocative protests that have stirred strong opinions on all sides.

Recent polls show that while many people view climate change as a major threat, support for climate policies doesn't necessarily translate to support for disruptive protests. A YouGov poll found that six in 10 Brits think defacing art or public monuments should be made a specific criminal offence.

However, some researchers argue that radical tactics deployed by small numbers of climate activists may be beneficial for wider progress, even if they are widely disliked by the public. Dana Fisher, a sociologist at the University of Maryland, explains that these actions aim to pull the conversation towards more extreme solutions and tactics.



Interestingly, studies suggest that dislike of protesters or their tactics doesn't appear to result in less support for climate action. Fisher notes that disruptive protests tend to generate more support for the issue, even if people don't like the organizations or tactics themselves.

However, the impact of protests on public opinion is mixed. Some studies have found that "extreme protest actions" can reduce support for a social movement's central cause, a situation often referred to as "the activist's dilemma."

Climate activists seeking to effect change may need to carefully balance getting attention for their issue without alienating potential supporters. The long-term impacts of such protests on both public opinion and policy remain an area of ongoing research and debate.

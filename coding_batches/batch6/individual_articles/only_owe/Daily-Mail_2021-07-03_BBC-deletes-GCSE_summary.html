BBC deletes GCSE revision guide that listed the UPSIDE of climate change after fury from environmental activists

Among the 'benefits' was the creation of new shipping routes due to melting ice. BBC later removed the 'positive' list and said it was not in line with the curriculum. Revision guide now only shows the negative impacts, such as rising sea levels.

The BBC has removed a list of 'positive impacts' of climate change from its Bitesize revision guide for GCSE geography students following intense backlash from environmental activists. The guide initially included controversial 'benefits' such as the creation of new shipping routes due to melting ice and lower heating bills due to warmer winters.

Campaigners, including Guardian journalist George Monbiot, branded the BBC Bitesize material an 'absolute disgrace'. Monbiot argued that the web page read like 'fossil fuel propaganda' and would leave schoolchildren thinking global warming was 'pretty good'.

The list of 'benefits' also included claims that warmer temperatures lead to 'healthier outdoor lifestyles', 'new tourist destinations', 'oil becoming available in Alaska and Siberia' as the ice melts, and some animals and plants may 'flourish'.

The broadcaster faced furious backlash from climate experts, campaigners, and teachers who said the arguments were 'flat wrong' and did not reflect the current syllabus. Stuart Lock, the chief executive of a group of schools in Bedfordshire, tweeted: "I think this is flat wrong, doesn't align with the national curriculum or exam specs, and needs reconsidering. Climate change isn't a 'both sides' argument."



Extinction Rebellion's south-east group stated: "GCSE students, young people, those facing future disasters, deserve better than to be judged on questions which warp and distort the truth."

In response to the outcry, the BBC said it decided to remove the list of 'positive' effects because it did not follow the national curriculum. The guide now shows only the negative impacts of global warming, such as rising sea levels, droughts, and greater risk of flooding.
